Yesterday's Lambeth Today

Public Housing Developments 1965-80

In the context of the UK’s current affordable housing shortage, many councils are replacing housing stock with new higher-density schemes. Architects are looking back to re-evaluate existing projects. Are there good examples that have stood the test of time?

The most recent period of extensive public housing construction is bookended by the 1965 reorganisation of local government and the beginning of the neo-liberal counter-revolution in the late 1970s. Many of its projects are still here; some have been demolished or face the threat of demolition. The geographical focus of this exhibition is Lambeth because during this period Lambeth’s Department for Architecture and Planning, under the leadership of Ted Hollamby, was developing an extensive variety of solutions in their search for high density.

Whilst in the 1960s a very positive attitude and trust in new technologies prevailed, the 1970s were marked by the global financial crisis, high inflation and unemployment levels.

In the 1960s the British government subsidised ‘high-rise’ construction, with new residential towers intended to demonstrate the power of the welfare state, examples of which in this exhibition are Lambeth’s pentagon and point blocks.

Scattered bomb-damaged sites were combined into large development areas, industry was moved, and houses between bought with the aid of Compulsory Purchase Orders (CPOs), all providing space for large estates. Examples of these are the Stockwell Park Estate, Myatt’s Fields (with its state-of-the-art services strategy manifested in the estate boiler house), and the Moorlands Estate, where Southwyck House was built to acoustically protect the residential development from the noise of the Greater London Council’s planned ring motorway, subsequently abandoned.

The 1960s also saw growing value being given to the refurbishment of existing accommodation and townscapes that people had identified with over generations. The designation of Conservation Areas, General Improvement Areas and Housing Action Areas from the late 1960s onwards indicates this as do many re-habilitation and infill projects. Community activism and community consultation developed in that period, here represented by Blenheim Gardens, Clapham Manor, and Central Hill, all examples of Lambeth’s low-rise high-density developments of which Cressingham Gardens and Dunbar Street are also notable.

The exhibition summarises the body of work investigated for a current University College London, Bartlett School of Architecture, Architectural and Urban History and Theory MPhil/PhD research project by Christiane Felber.
